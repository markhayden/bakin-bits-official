# Social Media Craft

How to make images that work on social. This is craft, not gospel — the platforms shift their algorithms quarterly and what worked last year is dead now. The principles below are durable; the specifics are not.

## The first three frames

A scroll passes a post in roughly 800ms on phone. The image has to do three things in that window:

1. **Stop the thumb.** Color contrast, an unexpected subject, a face making strong eye contact, motion implied through composition. Boring center-framed product shots lose every time.
2. **Promise something.** The viewer needs to think "I want to see what this is" within those 800ms. A close crop on a partial subject (food half-cut off, person mid-gesture) creates information gap. Don't show everything.
3. **Survive the platform's compression.** Instagram squashes contrast. TikTok adds heavy compression to images. Test by viewing on a 6" phone screen at 60% brightness — if it looks muddy, it's muddy.

## Click-bait without being slimy

Click-bait works because it triggers curiosity gaps. The slimy version uses fake gaps ("you won't believe…"); the legit version uses real ones. Differences:

- **Legit:** "I made this in 15 minutes" with a result that genuinely could be made in 15 minutes
- **Slimy:** "This one trick…" with no trick

Visually, both look similar — close crop, partial reveal, strong contrast. The difference is whether the post delivers on the implicit promise.

For Pixel: lean toward concrete promises. "How a $4 grocery-store ingredient makes the best pasta sauce" with a hero shot of the ingredient is honest click-bait. "You won't believe this pasta hack" with a generic plate photo is slimy and the algorithm has gotten good at down-ranking it.

## Platform aesthetics

**Instagram (feed):** Square or 4:5 vertical. Saturation slightly above natural. Lighting matters more than composition — a perfectly-lit boring photo beats a poorly-lit interesting one. Caption-driven posts can use plainer images.

**Instagram (Stories/Reels):** 9:16 vertical, full-bleed. Text overlays expected — leave 15% margin top and bottom for safe zones (clock + UI). Higher motion implied wins.

**TikTok:** 9:16 vertical, but TikTok's algorithm prefers images that look unfinished — handheld, slightly imperfect, lo-fi. Polished commercial photography reads as "ad" and gets skipped.

**Twitter/X:** 16:9 landscape or 1:1 square. Often viewed on desktop too. Text legibility is critical — many users have small viewports. Keep critical content away from the bottom 20% (UI overlap on mobile).

**LinkedIn:** 1.91:1 horizontal preferred. Professional but not generic. The algorithm rewards images of real people doing real work over stock-photo polish. Faces get more reach than product shots.

**Pinterest:** 2:3 vertical (1000x1500). Pin-shaped. Heavy text overlay on top half — Pinterest is essentially a search engine, not a feed. Keywords in the image matter.

## Composition cheats that earn the scroll-stop

- **Rule of thirds, broken intentionally.** Subjects in a strict third grid feel inert. Push the subject slightly off-grid — closer to a corner, partially out of frame. Discomfort registers as intentionality.
- **Negative space with purpose.** Half-empty frames with one strong element work — but only if the negative space leads the eye somewhere. Random empty space looks like a mistake.
- **Implied motion.** Even still images can imply movement: leaning subjects, blur on one side, cropped frames that suggest the subject is exiting. Static center-of-frame compositions read as boring.
- **Strong foreground/background separation.** Shallow depth of field on phone-scrolled feeds is the single biggest reach-multiplier. The eye locks onto the in-focus subject before the brain processes the background. Always blur the background unless the brief explicitly wants both planes sharp.

## What kills reach

- Logos, watermarks, or borders that scream "ad"
- Stock-photo lighting (flat, even, soft-box-everywhere)
- Faces that make awkward eye contact with the camera. Either committed eye contact or a clear elsewhere-glance — never the in-between.
- Text overlay in fonts that read "Microsoft Office clipart"
- More than 4 colors competing for attention. Pick a palette of 3 + one accent.
